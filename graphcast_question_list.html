1
研究背景：NWP的瓶颈与MLWP的机会
Global medium-range weather forecasting is critical to decision-making across many social and economic domains. Traditional numerical weather prediction (NWP) uses increased compute resources to improve forecast accuracy but does not directly use historical weather data to improve the underlying model.
The success of NWP lies in the rigorous and ongoing research practices that provide increasingly detailed descriptions of weather phenomena and in how well NWP scales to greater accuracy with greater computational resources. But while traditional NWP scales well with compute, capitalizing on the vast amount of historical weather data to improve accuracy is not straightforward. Rather, NWP methods are improved by highly trained experts innovating better models, algorithms, and approximations, which can be a time-consuming and costly process.
Machine learning–based weather prediction (MLWP)—wherein forecast models are trained from historical data—offers an alternative to traditional NWP. MLWP has the potential to improve forecast accuracy by capturing patterns in the data that are not easily represented in explicit equations. MLWP also offers opportunities for greater efficiency by exploiting modern deep learning hardware, rather than supercomputers, and striking more favorable speed–accuracy trade-offs.

2
GraphCast 系统：输入输出与核心设计
GraphCast takes as input the two most recent states of Earth's weather—the current time and 6 hours earlier—and predicts the next state of the weather 6 hours ahead. A single weather state is represented by a 0.25° latitude-longitude grid (721 by 1440), which corresponds to roughly 28 km by 28 km resolution at the equator, where each grid point represents a set of surface and atmospheric variables. Like traditional NWP systems, GraphCast is autoregressive: It can be "rolled out" by feeding its own predictions back in as input, to generate an arbitrarily long trajectory of weather states.
Here, we introduce GraphCast, which produces an accurate 10-day forecast in under a minute on a single Google Cloud TPU v4 device and supports applications including predicting tropical cyclone tracks, atmospheric rivers, and extreme temperatures.

3
架构与训练：Encoder-Processor-Decoder + ERA5
GraphCast is implemented as a neural network architecture, based on GNNs in an "encoder-processor-decoder" configuration, with a total of 36.7 million parameters.
The encoder maps variables represented as node attributes on the input grid to learned node attributes on an internal "multimesh" representation. The multimesh is a graph that is spatially homogeneous, with high spatial resolution over the globe. It is defined by refining a regular icosahedron iteratively six times. The processor uses 16 unshared GNN layers to perform learned message-passing on the multimesh, enabling efficient local and long-range information propagation. The decoder maps the final processor layer's learned features from the multimesh representation back to the latitude-longitude grid.
During model development, we used 39 years (1979–2017) of historical data from ECMWF's ERA5 reanalysis archive. As a training objective, we averaged the mean squared error (MSE) between GraphCast's predicted states over N autoregressive steps. The value of N was increased incrementally from 1 to 12 over the course of training. Consistent with real deployment scenarios, we evaluated GraphCast on the held-out data from the years 2018 onward.

对应问题2（架构三部分）
三部分功能拆解：
· Encoder：将经纬度网格数据"投影"到多尺度球面图（multimesh）节点上——相当于特征提取
· Processor：16层GNN在multimesh上做消息传递——捕获局部和远程大气相互作用
· Decoder：将multimesh特征映射回经纬度网格，输出预测增量（残差）

验证结果：公平比较与主要发现
In making these comparisons, skill was established on the basis of two key elements: (i) the selection of the ground truth for comparison and (ii) a careful accounting of the data assimilation windows used to infer this data from observations. We used ERA5 as the ground truth for evaluating GraphCast. However, evaluating HRES forecasts against ERA5 would result in nonzero error on the initial forecast step. Instead, we constructed an "HRES forecast at step 0" (HRES-fc0) dataset to use as ground truth for HRES.
GraphCast outperformed HRES on 90.3% of the 1380 targets and significantly (P ≤ 0.05, nominal sample size n ∈ {729, 730}) outperformed HRES on 89.9% of targets.
We found that increasing the number of autoregressive steps in the MSE loss also encourages GraphCast to blur to a degree at longer lead times, which means that its forecasts will lie somewhere between a traditional deterministic forecast and an ensemble mean. We found that RMSE-optimized blurring applied to GraphCast has greater skill than analogous blurring applied to HRES on 88.0% of our 1380 verification targets, which is generally consistent with our above conclusions.

5
极端事件预测：泛化能力的证明
Beyond evaluating GraphCast's forecast skill against HRES's, we also evaluated how its forecasts support predicting severe events, including tropical cyclones tracks, atmospheric rivers, and extreme temperature. These are key downstream applications for which GraphCast is not specifically trained, but which are very important for human activity.
GraphCast is significantly better than HRES for lead times of 18 hours to 4.75 days in tropical cyclone tracking. For atmospheric rivers, GraphCast improves the prediction of IVT compared with HRES, from 25% at short lead time to 10% at longer horizons.
GraphCast's precision-recall curves are above HRES's for 5- and 10-day lead times for extreme heat, suggesting GraphCast's forecasts are generally superior at extreme classification over longer horizons. By contrast, HRES has better precision-recall at the 12-hour lead time.

局限与结论：主动让步与历史定位
One key limitation of our approach is in how uncertainty is handled. We focused on deterministic forecasts and compared against HRES, but the ensemble forecasting system ENS is especially important for quantifying the probability of extreme events. The nonlinearity of weather dynamics means that there is increasing uncertainty at longer lead times, which is not well captured by a single deterministic forecast. Building probabilistic forecasts that model uncertainty more explicitly, along the lines of ensemble forecasts, is a crucial next step.
Our approach should not be regarded as a replacement for traditional weather forecasting methods, which have been developed for decades, rigorously tested in many real-world contexts. Rather, our work should be interpreted as evidence that MLWP is able to meet the challenges of real-world forecasting problems.
GraphCast's forecast skill and efficiency compared with HRES shows that MLWP methods are now competitive with traditional weather forecasting methods. We believe this marks a turning point in weather forecasting. Generally, GraphCast should be viewed as a family of models, with the current version being the largest we can practically fit under current engineering constraints, but which have the potential to scale much further in the future with greater compute resources and higher-resolution data.
